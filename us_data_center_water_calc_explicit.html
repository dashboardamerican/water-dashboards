RFS ethanol methodology & sources

This bar shows only the marginal irrigation water that would not exist without the Renewable Fuel Standard — not the total blue water embedded in all ethanol corn. Teter et al. (2018) modeled the Mandate scenario (RFS through 2030, ~15 Bgal/yr corn ethanol) vs. a no-biofuels counterfactual using coupled BEPAM economic + CropWatR biophysical models at 10 km resolution. The +729 billion liters/year net irrigation increase (≈ 192 Bgal/year) is concentrated in Kansas, Nebraska, Oklahoma, and Texas — regions that source >90% of their irrigation from the Ogallala Aquifer, a fossil groundwater system with negligible natural recharge. The same mandate also reduced groundwater infiltration by 587 billion liters/year, compounding the aquifer stress.

This is the most conservative estimate available: the total blue water of all US ethanol corn (1.0–1.4 trillion gal/yr before co-product allocation) is 5–7× larger. Convergent support: Lark et al. (PNAS 2022) found the RFS expanded corn acreage by 2.8 Mha with corn prices up ~31%; Sampson et al. (2021) showed ethanol markets drove ~4% of total Kansas irrigated water use at the field level, scaling consistently to the Teter national estimate.

Co-product note: DDGS (distillers grains) is a byproduct of ethanol, not the reverse — the RFS mandate drives the corn demand. Under energy-based LCA allocation, 57% of crop water is attributed to ethanol. The 192 Bgal marginal figure avoids the allocation question entirely by measuring only the net increase caused by the policy.

Disclosure gap — why some industries are missing

Several major US industrial water consumers (chemical manufacturing, steelmaking, mining, oil refining) are excluded because no current, primary-sourced consumptive-use data exists. The Census Bureau's Survey of Water Use in Manufacturing was defunded after 1983; USGS does not break out industrial water by subsector; and these industries do not voluntarily report consumption. Data centers are among the only US industrial water consumers with transparent, audited, annually reported water data.

Methods Summary

Scope: 2023 values are source-reported. The default 2025 headline uses a buildout-calibrated point estimate (IEA + CBRE scaling). A separate LBNL 2028-bound interpolation range is retained below as a sensitivity view. The US denominator uses the latest available USGS modeled CONUS consumptive-use values (water year 2020).

Buildout Assumptions Used for 2025 Default

  • 2023 baseline numerators: 66B L/year direct and 800B L/year indirect (LBNL).
  • Default tracked signed PPA capacity baseline (strict US renewables PPA-focused, big 4): 59.955 GW = 6.485 (Google strict US renewable PPA set, conservative curated) + 20.043 (Amazon US off-site wind/solar floor after subtracting up to 0.061 GW on-site) + 9.800 (Meta US wind/solar contract-backed projects) + 23.627 (Microsoft US map-derived contracted value 26.252 GW x 90% renewables-PPA proxy).
  • 2024 anchor: TWh implied vs 176 TWh 2023 baseline, giving x.
  • 2025 buildout step: +% key-US-market inventory growth (CBRE), giving x.
  • Combined 2023 to 2025 scale: x applied to direct and selected-boundary indirect numerators.
  • US denominator remains ~95,143.8 kAF/year (derived from USGS PP1894-D modeled 2020 values).

Not modeled here: water replenishment. This calculator shows gross consumption only. Major data-center operators are among the few US industrial water consumers with active replenishment programs: Google replenished 4.5B gal in 2024 (~64% of consumption; 112 projects, 68 watersheds); Meta replenished 1.5B gal in 2024; Microsoft committed to zero-water cooling on all new builds (Aug 2024); AWS operates at 0.15 L/kWh WUE (lowest among the big four). Comparable replenishment or offset commitments are not standard practice in peer sectors shown in the industry comparison above (golf courses, pulp/paper, swimming pools, agriculture).

RFS ethanol — Ogallala aquifer concentration

”net increases in irrigation water requirements occur in regions of Texas, Oklahoma, and Kansas that source the vast majority (>90%) of their irrigation from the Ogallala Aquifer, a resource from which water is currently being drawn at unsustainable rates”

Teter et al. (2018) p. 12. The Mandate scenario shifts irrigation demand to the Great Plains (Ogallala region) even as it reduces irrigated acreage nationally by 2%, because increased corn in Kansas/Nebraska/Oklahoma requires more water per acre than the crops it displaces.
 Teter et al. (2018), p. 12
RFS land-use change — corn expansion and price impacts

”the RFS increased corn prices by 30% and the prices of other crops by 20%, which, in turn, expanded US corn cultivation by 2.8 Mha (8.7%) and total cropland by 2.1 Mha (2.4%)”

Lark et al. (2022) abstract. The 2.8 Mha of additional corn (95% CI: 2.4–3.1 Mha) includes 2.1 Mha increase in continuous corn and 1.4 Mha additional corn in rotation. Expansion concentrated in North/South Dakota, western Minnesota, Mississippi Alluvial Plain, and High Plains (KS, OK, TX).
